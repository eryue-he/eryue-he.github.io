Mabinogion

The Mabinogion consists of a multitude of stories that are quite entertaining to read. The stories are not sequential, instead they consist of discrete tales covering various subjects. In this page I will be recording down notes that I found useful when reading, usually that's not a thing that I do but the setting of Albion is relatively foreign to me despite having some exposure before as a youth to its ancient and medieval history. Historical events, cataclysmic shifts, major figures, I surely must know some, on the other hand cultural aspects such as folklore remain a mystery to me.

Owain son of Urien (Owain mab Urien)
Owain is referred to as Owain son of Urien (Urien Rheged), in the tale there are quite a few references to real life figures, mythologized versions, and it all blurs together such as the inclusion of King Arthur. Owain historically was a king of North Rheged, where at the apex of its strength, they faced off against the kingdom of Bernicia (Angles) with Owain's most notable battles being that of the Battle of Alclud Ford and Argoed Llwyfain. Our character here however is portrayed more like a one man hero, one can say a romantic whom goes on quests and faces off against tough opposition whether it be the environment, broken promises, his questing or his whole redemption arc. Owain most notably has been immortalized thanks to a poet by the name of Taliesin in the poem "Gweith Argoed Llwyfain". The most notable part of the poem goes like this:

Fflamddwyn is an Angles, one of the leaders of the opposing warband that was slain by Owain in the battle of Argoed Llwyfain as retold in the poem above.

King Arthur (Brenin Arthur)
King Arthur is frequently mentioned in medieval romances (literature and performance) well beyond that of the Mabinogion. One of the theories behind the "real life figure" is that he was a Romano-British warlord whom rallied the Romano-British and Celts to resist Anglo-Saxon incursions. In the context of the Welsh tales he is in charge of Camelot. Within Welsh tales he ranges from that of a warrior king to a magical figure. In French tales his role shifts from that have a warrior to one of primarily "doing nothing" where his inactivity leads to a flaw in an otherwise ideal Camelot. Given the variety of interpretations and tales from various authors, our character is sure to be varied, in The Mabinogion we have more of Geoffrey of Monmouth's influence which leans more towards the warrior king aspect.

Caerlleon Upon Usk
Caer-Leon (City of Legions)
This is an old Roman legionary fortress, Romans when setting up fortifications like to establish all kinds of infrastructure that offer various ameneties akin to that of more permanent U.S. military bases so you'd have things such as taverns, baths, amphitheatres, offices, residential areas, granaries, etc. Given this, one will probably understand how Caerlleon Upon Usk makes for a comfortable position for Arthur and the whole Camelot group.
