Compost, Soil, Florida and What Not

Some notes I recorded down from talking to various people in tandem with some information I scoured on my own related to the locality here.

Compost

Ratios - Approximately <30 nitrogen, >30 carbon.
Layer 50/50 ontop of rough base of sticks, ideally hay or dry grass, add bits of soil when you can Worms + isopods, ideally put them in to help process the compost.
Compost high in carbon entails: autumn leaves, straw, woodchips, sawdust, etc etc.
Compost high in nitrogen entails: vegtable scraps, coffee grounds, manure, etc.
Since I never passed chemistry, just remember that in terms of pH levels, low = acid, high = base, middle = neutral. Dimwit like me has to remember somehow lol.
Flowers that counteract some pests include, lavender & rosemary.


Remember to dig out the roots of wild stuff, always keep digging, pulling, keep things clear! :).

Florida Soil
Most Floridian soils have low organic material, typically much less than 5%, in my locality this is fairly accurate, generally what I observe in smaller scale gardens is the heavy usage of compost, additional soil bought elsewhere, ready made compost, and so forth (not counting large scale agriculture). Generally there is a "structureless" type of soil here, therefor it is more efficient long term (I'm too busy atm) to improve the soil structure within where one is growing crops. This solves the root of the issue and if you happen to have the time and resources, it is ideal. With better soil structure you can allow roots to penetrate better without having to deal with too much clay or giant rocks, etc. In our case Florida soil has high bulk density, compost and things of that nature is ideal. The proper classification of the soil should be spodosol or something along those lines which pretty much entails poor drainange, sand, clay, acid, etc. With low base saturation (< 35%) we are in for some pain, besides that however the weather and moisture is sufficient for growing crops, well at least the scallions grow alright for years at this point.
